Outrage as Disney successfully trademarks Hakuna Matata phrase

- The phrase became popular in 1984 after in was used in a hit song by Mushrooms

- Disney released a movie in 1994 with the same phrase and applied to patent it

- The trademark was issued in 2003 allowing Disney to sue anyone who used it without permissiom

- A Zimbabwean citizen has filed a petition seeking to compel Disney to drop the trademark

- Social media users, via a viral Twitter hashtag hakuna matata have strongly critisised the film maker for the action

American film production company Disney has for over a week now been on the receiving end after reports emerged it fully owned the famous Swahili phrase 'Hakuna Matata'.

The development has attracted bruising social media criticism from Swahili speaking countries for instance Kenya, Tanzania and Uganda among others form East and Central Africa.

What has, however, stood out is a petition by a Zimbabwean activist, Shelton Mpala, who seeks to compel Disney to drop the trademark.

The petition has since garnered over 38,500 signatures from those supporting the course.

Mpala has described the move by Disney as 'colonialism and robbery' since the phrase is largely associated with Kenyans.

"I liken this to colonialism and robbery, the appropriation of something you have no right over. Imagine, If we were to go that route, then we owe the British royalties for everyone who speaks English, or France for when we speak French," the petition reads.

TUKO.co.ke understands that the film producer publicised its intention to own the phrase in 1994.

In 2001, it was published for opposition and in 2003, it was registered as a legal trademark.

The phrase was used in its famous animation film dubbed as Lion King which was first aired in August 1994.

The trade mark prevents anyone from publishing the Swahili word 'Hakuna Matata' without permission. It gives Disney powers to sue anyone who violates the trade mark.

Those who reacted on the matter took a jab at the American company stating that their it was unacceptable for them to claim ownership of an African word.

Others have critisised leaders from Kenya and Tanzania (majority Swahili speakers) for staying mum of the subject and leaving the matter to a non Swahili speaker.

"I am not surprised. We can't even fight for Migingo. Now foreigners walk in and steal Kenyan brands and patent them for themselves. All the while we are too busy stealing to notice," twitted Lord Abraham Mutai."Hakuna Matata' is a Kenyan brand. Our Government has refused to fight for it because it is busy, rewarding criminals with hefty state jobs. Forcing citizens to pay taxes for services that will never be delivered," reacted a tweep identified as Eric.
